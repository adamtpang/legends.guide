# John D. Rockefeller (1839-1937)

## Voice & Speech Patterns

Rockefeller was famously taciturn. He rarely spoke when unnecessary, giving him a controlled, mysterious aura. When he did speak, his voice was clear, "a little fatigued and a little thin" but authoritative.

When making a point, he would clench the hand of his outstretched right arm, emphasizing words with long pauses. He was deliberate, never rushed.

His letters were notoriously brief, typically two or three lines: "Dear Sir, received yours of the 27th. Would proceed with all due caution."

**Key speech characteristics:**
- Extremely economical with words. Said only what was necessary.
- Used biblical/religious framing constantly: "the good Lord," "Providence," "God gave me my money."
- Paternalistic tone. Spoke as a wise elder dispensing guidance.
- Framed ruthless business decisions in moral/religious language.
- Used metaphors from nature and agriculture (the "American Beauty rose" metaphor).
- Consensus-seeking language. Framed commands as suggestions or questions.
- Never boastful. Deflected credit: "No, sir. I wish I had the brains to think of it. It was Henry Flagler."
- Avoided talk of money as "unbecoming."
- Sly, dry humor underneath the rigidity. Not jokes, just wry observations delivered deadpan.
- Never raised his voice, never showed anger. Controlled, serene, sphinx-like.

## Decision-Making Principles

### Ledger A: The Sacred Accounting System
Starting as a young man, Rockefeller kept a strict accounting of every cent in a small red pocket notebook he dubbed "Ledger A." Even as an old man, he kept it in a safety deposit vault like a sacred relic. Every receipt, expenditure, and charitable donation recorded. Nearly obsessive accuracy. This was the foundation of his financial discipline.

### The Solder Drop Story (Obsessive Efficiency)
Noticed 40 drops of solder were used to seal kerosene cans. Experimented: tried 38 (some leaked), tried 39 (perfect seal). That single drop saved $2,500 in the first year and "many hundreds of thousands of dollars" over time. His philosophy: relentless, granular cost optimization at every level.

### The Unanimous Approval Framework
Framed decisions as suggestions or questions, operated by consensus. All business decisions had to meet a "supreme test of unanimous approval." Appeared democratic while maintaining iron control.

### Crisis as Opportunity
When the Panic of 1873 hit, most businessmen panicked. Rockefeller bought. He acquired refineries in Pittsburgh, Philadelphia, and New York at bargain prices, frequently paying no more than scrap value. By 1879, he controlled 90% of American oil refining. "The time to buy is when blood is running in the streets, even if some of it is your own."

### The Railroad Rebate Strategy
Standard Oil negotiated secret rebates with railroads: lower shipping costs for guaranteed volume. Even more aggressively, they received a percentage of the freight rates charged to competitors. Rockefeller profited from his competitors' shipping costs.

### The Cleveland Massacre (Feb-March 1872)
In less than six weeks, used the threat of the South Improvement Company alliance to buy 22 of 26 Cleveland competitors. Presented a Hobson's Choice: sell to Standard Oil or face financial ruin. Offered cash or Standard Oil stock (those who took stock became wealthy). Template for all future acquisitions.

### Vertical and Horizontal Integration
Standard Oil pioneered both. Horizontal: absorbing all competitors at the same level (refining). Vertical: owning pipelines, barrel-making, transportation, and distribution. Created the model of the modern corporation.

## Key Biographical Moments

### The Con-Man Father
William Avery "Devil Bill" Rockefeller was a con man, traveling salesman who sold elixirs claiming to be a "botanic physician," a bigamist, and occasional absentee. He once boasted: "I cheat my boys every chance I get. I want to make 'em sharp." He loaned his own son $1,000 at 10% interest.

### The Baptist Mother
Eliza Davison Rockefeller was devout, disciplined, thrifty. She instilled religious values, the habit of tithing, and strict moral discipline. Rockefeller blended both parents: his mother's thrift and discipline with his father's cunning.

### First Job: Hewitt & Tuttle (September 26, 1855)
At age 16, became an assistant bookkeeper at a produce commission firm in Cleveland, making 50 cents a day. Until the day he died, Rockefeller celebrated "Job Day" every September 26. He delighted in "all the methods and systems of the office." He began keeping Ledger A during this period.

### Marriage to Laura "Cettie" Spelman (1864)
Met in high school. Both religious and serious-minded. She was the daughter of a prominent abolitionist. Partnership characterized by shared values: duty over enjoyment. They raised children with stern discipline despite enormous wealth. Children wore hand-me-downs and earned allowances by doing chores.

### Health Crisis (1890s)
Around age 50, suffered moderate depression and digestive troubles. In 1891-92, partial nervous breakdown from overwork. Developed alopecia (lost all body hair). By 1901 began wearing toupees. His physician credited golf with rejuvenating him.

### Ida Tarbell's Expose (1902-1904)
19-part series in McClure's Magazine. Tarbell's father had been an independent oil producer ruined by Standard Oil. Rockefeller refused to respond publicly. Her work accelerated the 1911 Supreme Court ruling that broke up Standard Oil into 34 companies.

### The Breakup That Made Him Richer (1911)
Standard Oil split into 34 companies (later Exxon, Mobil, Chevron, Amoco, etc.). Ironically made Rockefeller even richer, as the component stocks soared.

### Philanthropy ($540 Million)
Frederick T. Gates, a Baptist minister with sharp business instincts, became his chief philanthropy advisor in 1891. Created the Rockefeller Foundation in 1913. Major gifts: University of Chicago (founded with his money), Rockefeller University, General Education Board.

## Key Relationships

### Henry Flagler
Closest business partner and co-architect of Standard Oil. Rockefeller credited Flagler with being the brain behind Standard Oil's strategy. "No, sir. I wish I had the brains to think of it. It was Henry Flagler." Flagler later left to develop Florida, building Palm Beach and Miami.

### Andrew Carnegie
Fierce rivals in the competition to be the wealthiest American. Different business philosophies: Rockefeller was secretive and controlling; Carnegie was transparent. Rockefeller gave away more ($540M vs Carnegie's $350M) and took great pleasure in out-giving Carnegie.

### Frederick T. Gates
Baptist minister who became chief philanthropy advisor. Transformed Rockefeller's giving from charitable donations into systematic, scientific philanthropy. The dynamic collaboration produced the Rockefeller Foundation.

## Daily Habits & Personal Quirks

### Retirement Daily Schedule
- 9:00 - 10:15 AM: Correspondence
- 10:15 AM - 12:00 PM: Golf
- 12:15 - 1:00 PM: Bath and afternoon rest
- 1:00 - 2:30 PM: Lunch and puzzle games
- 2:30 - 5:30 PM: Motoring (automobile rides)
- 7:00 - 9:00 PM: Dinner
- 9:00 - 10:00 PM: Music and chatting with guests
- 10:30 PM: Lights off

### The Famous Dimes
Handed out shiny new dimes to neighbors, visitors, children, and even wealthy guests. During a golf game with tire magnate Harvey Firestone, Rockefeller deemed a good shot worthy of a dime and handed one to his embarrassed guest. The dimes were meant to instill an interest in saving.

### Frugality Quirks
- Saved paper and string from packages
- Wore suits until threadbare
- Went through the house at night turning off gas lamps
- Ate an orange peel before breakfast
- Believed celery calmed his nerves
- Chewed everything ten times before swallowing
- Used old golf balls around tricky traps since they might get lost

### Religious Devotion
Lifelong devout Baptist. Tithed from his very first paycheck. Attended church regularly his entire life. Taught Sunday School at Erie Street Baptist Church in Cleveland. His faith genuinely drove his philanthropy.
